| <a href="https://youtu.be/IK4BGi8PxBU?t=1257" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">20:57</a> | How should one respond to the early-date argument that Exodus 14:2 toponyms could have existed earlier even if texts preserving them have not survived? | He says that might work for some Exodus 14:2 names, but it fails for broader Exodus toponyms such as Migdol, Pithom, and Ramesses, whose historical settings are later. |
| <a href="https://youtu.be/IK4BGi8PxBU?t=1670" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">27:50</a> | Why did Samson and the prince of Shechem depend on their fathers to arrange their marriages? | First marriages were normally arranged by parents in the ancient Near East; arranging a good match was treated as responsible parenting. |
| <a href="https://youtu.be/IK4BGi8PxBU?t=2101" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">35:01</a> | Was Ezekiel 29:17-21 unfulfilled, and could Persia have taken Babylon's role? | He says Nebuchadnezzar did take Ty, and the prophecy involves Babylon and Egypt rather than Persia replacing Babylon's role. |
| <a href="https://youtu.be/IK4BGi8PxBU?t=2625" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">43:45</a> | What likely happened to Sennacherib's army in Isaiah 37:36? | He thinks a fast-moving camp disease, specifically cholera from contaminated water and poor sanitation, is the best explanation. |
| <a href="https://youtu.be/IK4BGi8PxBU?t=3024" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">50:24</a> | In 1 Chronicles 7:21, were Ephraim's sons stealing livestock in Gath while they should have been in Egypt? | No. He reads the passage as later clan history after the conquest and says the Gathites, not Ephraim's descendants, were probably the livestock thieves. |
| <a href="https://youtu.be/IK4BGi8PxBU?t=3396" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">56:36</a> | Was there a charge for being drunk and disorderly in the ancient Near East? | Usually no by itself; it became punishable when drunkenness led to other crimes or serious public disorder. |
| <a href="https://youtu.be/IK4BGi8PxBU?t=3542" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">59:02</a> | What are the biggest problems with the Levite hypothesis? | He says it depends heavily on the documentary hypothesis, assumes an implausibly tiny exodus and quiet infiltration, and does not fit destruction evidence such as Jericho and Hazor. |
| <a href="https://youtu.be/IK4BGi8PxBU?t=3903" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">1:05:03</a> | If the exodus group was only about 2,000 people, would Pharaoh even have cared? | He says no, and also argues that such a small group would not realistically be enough to conquer places like Jericho on its own. |
| <a href="https://youtu.be/IK4BGi8PxBU?t=4121" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">1:08:41</a> | Are cursed objects such as dolls or books a real thing? | He treats the subject cautiously, saying the evidence is too thin to make sweeping claims and that the term "cursed object" covers several different ideas. |
| <a href="https://youtu.be/IK4BGi8PxBU?t=4679" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">1:17:59</a> | If you found a newly opened tomb with an ancient curse on it, would you open it? | Yes; he says the so-called mummy's curse is fake folklore rather than something archaeologists should treat as real. |
| <a href="https://youtu.be/IK4BGi8PxBU?t=4801" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">1:20:01</a> | Did Egyptian tomb robbers ignore tomb curses, or just fail to read them? | He says most Egyptian tomb curses were not really anti-entry traps at all, but ritual-purity warnings in tombs that were often meant to receive public offerings. |
| <a href="https://youtu.be/IK4BGi8PxBU?t=4911" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">1:21:51</a> | How is possession different from mental illness? | He says symptoms can overlap, so mental illness should be assessed first; the clearest distinction would be preternatural abilities such as unknown languages or levitation. |
| <a href="https://youtu.be/IK4BGi8PxBU?t=5133" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">1:25:33</a> | What criteria are used to differentiate alleged demonic possession from mental or neurological disorder? | He treats it as more pastoral than scholarly and again points to preternatural abilities as the main marker. |
| <a href="https://youtu.be/IK4BGi8PxBU?t=5378" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">1:29:38</a> | Do modern governments contact spiritual beings? | Not routinely; he distinguishes possession from influence and says governments may be spiritually influenced through ordinary moral corruption. |
| <a href="https://youtu.be/IK4BGi8PxBU?t=5872" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">1:37:52</a> | Did the loss of Egyptian chariots in the exodus contribute to the later weakening of dynasties 19 and 20? | Yes; he argues chariot corps take decades to rebuild because they require horses, wood imports, and long training pipelines. |
| <a href="https://youtu.be/IK4BGi8PxBU?t=6534" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">1:48:54</a> | Did Jeroboam get the idea for his cult images from the Sinai golden calf? | No; he says Jeroboam's bull imagery fits Baal worship rather than a reuse of the Sinai calf episode. |
| <a href="https://youtu.be/IK4BGi8PxBU?t=7499" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">2:04:59</a> | How can one determine whether the early chapters of Genesis are factual history, hyperbole, metaphor, or something else? | He says hermeneutics there is hard and requires attention to ancient Near Eastern ritual, sacred space, and the foundation those chapters provide for tabernacle theology. |

| <a href="https://youtu.be/IK4BGi8PxBU?t=8464" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">2:21:04</a> | Could Ramesses II's arthritis explain why he was not personally at the Reed Sea incident? | Possibly; he says arthritis is one reasonable explanation, especially since an older king would not necessarily remain an active chariot campaigner. |
